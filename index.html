What the chart shows

The chart plots three things for each sprint:

  • Commitment (orange) — how many points the team took into the sprint
  • Completed (blue) — how many points were actually delivered
  • Velocity (purple) — a 3-sprint rolling average of Completed, giving a smoothed view of the team's sustainable pace

The shaded area between Commitment and Completed tells the story at a glance: green means the team delivered more than it committed to; red means it fell short.

Why it matters for a Scrum team

Sprint planning accuracy. Consistently red shading means the team is over-committing — taking in more work than it can reliably finish. This leads to carry-over, incomplete stories, and pressure to cut corners. Consistently green shading can indicate under-committing, which leaves capacity on the table. The goal is a narrow band close to 100%.

Velocity as a planning tool. The purple velocity line smooths out the sprint-to-sprint noise and shows the team's true sustainable pace. Sprint planning should be anchored to this number, not to a single outlier sprint. If Commitment is consistently above the velocity line, the team will keep under-delivering.

Spotting trends early. A widening red gap over several sprints is an early warning sign — scope is growing faster than capacity, or something is regularly disrupting delivery. A narrowing gap shows the team is getting better at forecasting and execution.

The Ratio column in the table gives the per-sprint number (Completed ÷ Commitment as a percentage), making it easy to spot individual outlier sprints alongside the chart's overall trend.
